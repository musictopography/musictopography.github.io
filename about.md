### Music in Milan


The main objective of the project is to <b>reconstruct the complex relationship between music and space</b> at a moment when Milan was not only the epicentre of the Italian “economic miracle”, but also the driving force behind musical transformations at the national level.

In the short span from 1958 to 1962, <b>Milan was the cradle of the Italian recording industry</b>: it was here that Dischi Ricordi, founded in 1958 under the direction of Nanni Ricordi, produced the records of the first “cantautori”, such as Gino Paoli, Umberto Bindi, and Giorgio Gaber.

In the same years, <b>Milan was “the city of jazz”</b>, home to the magazine Musica Jazz and to musicians of national and international renown, including Gianni Basso, Oscar Valdambrini, and Franco Cerri.

<b>Italian rock and roll also took shape in Milan</b>: it was in Milanesse nightclubs and sports arenas that future protagonists of the genre such as Adriano Celentano and Tony Dallara first emerged.

<b>A widespread interest in popular and dialect repertoires</b> culminated in the success of the show Milanin Milanon, staged at the Teatro Gerolamo at the end of 1962; in the same year, Roberto Leydi and Gianni Bosio founded the <b> Nuovo Canzoniere Italiano</b>.

The Teatro Gerolamo was also a <b>key venue for the “canzone intellettuale”</b> and hosted several of its most important manifestations, such as Laura Betti’s Giro a vuoto in January 1960, with music by Luciano Chailly, Gino Negri, and Pietro Umiliani. Negri’s own experimental works were staged at the Teatro Gerolamo as well. It was he, together with Fiorenzo Carpi, who composed the music for the productions of the Piccolo Teatro.

Finally, <b>Italy’s first electronic music production studio</b>, the Studio di fonologia musicale della RAI, was experiencing one of its most significant phases during the same period.



### A forgotten city


In the face of such a variety of impulses, <b>almost no trace of that music scene remains in either the physical or the cultural landscape of Milan</b>. Investigating the musical practices of the economic boom through the lens of space thus offers a way to bring back to light a cultural heritage that has been lost over time, buried beneath the successive layers of an ever-changing city.


### Methodology


The methodological approach of the research is based on <b>“deep mapping”</b>, a practice grounded in the synergy between visual maps, computational data analysis, and fieldwork.

The reference dataset brings together <b>8,288 musical events, described through 53 features</b>; it is built on information drawn from two of the major newspapers of the time, «Il Giorno» and «Il Corriere della Sera».

These data are geographically visualised in interactive digital maps, programmed in «R». The same dataset was also used to create, through the software Gephi, three network graphs showing the reticular structure of the relationships between venues and music genres, venues and performers, and performers and composers.

<b>Both forms of visualisation were used to catalyse the memories of eight musicians active at the time</b>. The audio-video materials produced through these meetings are displayed in a [dedicated digital map](https://www.musictopography.com/maps/music_memories.html), creating a <b>feedback-based synergy between quantitative and qualitative tools </b>. 

All the materials produced during the research ([maps](https://www.musictopography.com/maps/), [network graphs](https://www.musictopography.com/network_graphs/), graphs and [interviews](https://www.musictopography.com/interviews/) are accessible through dedicated pages.
